Object of the University Library The man did not wait for the digital to try to overcome his weaknesses or infirmities. Thus the first dentures identified date from 5000av. J.-C. Since then, progress and innovations have taken place. In their article, he devotes a specialized section on augmented man. He retraces digital medicine throughout the centuries. He explains that in 3000 B.C. the Egyptians made wooden feet and toes to equip amputees. In the 16th century, the French surgeon Ambroise Paré took up the issue of orthopedic prostheses. With the help of a locksmith and a watchmaker, this enlightened mind brings advanced improvements to articulated prostheses to mechanically reproduce the natural movement of the limbs. In 1727, the British optician Scarlett developed a revolutionary concept: glasses with rigid branches finished by rings ensuring a perfect fit. Then in 187, the American John Wesley Hyatt discovers a revolutionary material, celluloid, a very light plastic that allows the shapes of frames never seen before and contributes to the decrease of the cost price. Most advanced prostheses use motors controlled by myoelectric signals sent by the brain to the muscles above the amputated limb. The bionic arm designed in 2005 for Claudia Mitchell, a former American Navy, meets another logic. Designed by surgeons at the Rehabilitation Institute of Chicago, the prosthesis communicates with the brain, a chip that analyzes a hundred neuronal signals and controls the 22 distinct functions of the prosthesis. In 2020, a team of American-Hong Kong scientists developed a bionic eye called EC-EYE. This prototype consists of tiny sensors that mimic the photoreceptor cells of the human eye. Arranged on a semi-spherical aluminum and tungsten membrane less than three centimeters in diameter, the sensors combine to reconstitute the retina.
